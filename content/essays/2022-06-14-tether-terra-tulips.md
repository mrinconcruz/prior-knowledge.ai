***The following essay expands on a June 13, 2022, Wall Street Journal column co-authored with Niall Ferguson, found [here](https://www.wsj.com/articles/stablecoins-infect-financial-markets-janet-yellen-terra-luna-tether-crypto-run-risk-collapse-recession-investment-dollar-stability-inflation-11655057043), which in turn adapts a Buttonwood Letter distributed on May 11, 2022 as Terra was de-pegging.***

***TL;DR: Stablecoins, as they exist today, do not present classic systemic risks to the financial system.***
-	***Collateralized stablecoins do not have run risks, since only a select number of institutions can redeem USDC and USDT and Circle and Tether can suspend redemptions at any time.***
-	***Coupon coins schemes like Terra constitute speculative gambling games between big whales, much like tulip mania centuries ago. So while they can produce losses for investors (like any investment) they do not pose systemic risk.***

***Regulators would be wise to allow stablecoins to develop without systemic risks, rather than encourage bank-like features—like demand redemption—which create systemic fragility.***


## Introduction

The precipitous fall of the “stablecoin” Terra has re-energized crypto skeptics. On May 10, 2022, the morning after Terra’s collapse, Treasury Secretary Janet Yellen argued before the Senate that stablecoins create “run risks which could threaten financial stability – risks associated with the payment system and its integrity."[^1] Clearly, not every token that calls itself as a “stablecoin” is stable. But Yellen is wrong to think that stablecoins pose a systemic risk to financial stability.

## Golden Tethers & Collateralized Stablecoins

Among true stablecoins, collateralized coins such as Tether’s USDT or Circle’s USDC loom largest at $74BN and $53BN in circulating supply.[^2] Opportunistic regulators and politicians, notably the SEC’s Gary Gensler and Senator Elizabeth Warren, often call these stablecoins “wildcat banks,” arguing that they are susceptible to runs (and ignoring research by George Selgin and Larry White, which debunks the whole idea of wildcat banks).[^3]

But Tether and Circle are quite different from banks. Unlike bank deposits, USDT and USDC are redeemable only in amounts exceeding $100,000 and only to a select group of Tether’s and Circle’s financial partners.[^4] Even then, Tether and Circle can delay or suspend redemptions at any time—history’s time-tested solution to runs.[^5]  

More importantly, while customers can redeem deposits only with their bank, holders of USDT and USDC can easily sell their tokens. This leaves Tether’s and Circle’s balance sheets intact and, to the chagrin of Twitter commentators, makes it impossible to “short” Tether or Circle by attacking their dollar pegs.

The only real risk to Tether and Circle is not bank runs but losses on their holdings of American (and possibly Chinese) bonds. On May 12, 2022, Tether CTO Paolo Ardoino clarified that “in the last six months, [Tether has] reduced 50% the size of the commercial papers (sic). Everything that was reduced from the commercial paper was rolled into U.S. Treasuries.”[^7] If so, this would mean that Treasuries are now 60% of reserves, and commercial paper 15%.[^8] Circle for its part claims that, as of May 20, 2022, 76% of its reserves are in Treasuries and 24% in cash.[^9]
